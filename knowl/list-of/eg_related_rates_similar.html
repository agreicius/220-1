Example 18.3. Using similar triangles.

A light shines from the top of a pole 20 meters high. A ball is dropped from the same height at a point 12 meters away from the light pole. How fast is the shadow of the ball moving along the ground \(1/2\) second later? Assume the ball falls a distance of \(10\, t^2\) meters in \(t\) seconds.
Solution.
We define
\begin{align*} h(t) \amp = \begin{array}{l} \text{height of ball}\\ t \text{ seconds after drop} \end{array}\\ s(t) \amp = \begin{array}{l} \text{dist. btwn. shadow and where ball lands}\\ t \text{ seconds after drop} \end{array}\text{.} \end{align*}
Since after \(t\) seconds the ball has dropped \(10\, t^2\) meters, and since its original height is 20 meters, we have
\begin{equation*} h(t)=20-10\, t^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
Next using the two similar right triangles in Figure 18.4, we see that
\begin{equation} \frac{h(t)}{20}=\frac{s(t)}{s(t)+12}\text{.}\tag{18.2} \end{equation}
We wish to compute \(s'(1/2)\text{.}\) To do so we take the derivative of both sides of (18.2):
\begin{equation*} \frac{h'(t)}{20}=\frac{s'(t)(s(t)+12)-s(t)s'(t)}{(s(t)+12)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
From this it follows that
\begin{equation*} s'(t)=\frac{h'(t)(s(t)+12)^2}{240}=-\frac{t}{12}(s(t)+12)^2\text{,} \end{equation*}
where we have used the fact that \(h'(t)=-2\cdot 10\, t=-20\, t\text{.}\) Lastly, to compute \(s'(1/2)\text{,}\) we need to compute \(s(1/2)\text{.}\) This is determined by evaluating (18.2) at \(t=1/2\) and solving for \(s(1/2)\text{.}\) Since \(h(1/2)=20-10\cdot 1/4=35/2\text{,}\) we have
\begin{align*} \frac{h(1/2)}{20}=\frac{s(1/2)}{s(1/2)+12} \amp \implies \frac{35/2}{20}=\frac{s(1/2)}{s(1/2)+12} \\ \amp \implies \frac{35}{40}(s(1/2)+12)=s(1/2)\\ \amp \implies \frac{84}{8}=\frac{1}{8}s(1/2)\\ \amp \implies s(1/2)=84\text{.} \end{align*}
We conclude that
\begin{align*} s'(1/2) \amp =-\frac{1/2}{12}(84+12)^2\\ \amp = -\frac{96^2}{24}\\ \amp = -\frac{1152}{5}\\ \amp = -384\text{.} \end{align*}
We conclude that \(1/2\)-second after the drop the ball’s shadow is traveling at a rate of 384 meters per second toward the lamp.
